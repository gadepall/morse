\section{Introduction}
Wireless optical communication is highly dependent on clear Line-of-Sight (LoS) and a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) \cite{elgala2011, ghassemlooy2012}. In low-cost embedded systems, a standard Light Emitting Diode (LED) paired with a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) is commonly used to demonstrate data transfer. However, ambient environmental lighting introduces a severe DC bias into the receiver, and LDRs exhibit significant photo-conductive latency \cite{sewaiwar2015}. 
The novelty of this paper lies in its evolutionary approach to solving physical layer limitations, building upon foundational On-Off Keying (OOK) optical paradigms \cite{komine2004}. We first establish a baseline VLC system to document hardware-induced signal degradation, known as the "Iceberg Effect." Following this, we introduce an advanced Infrared (IR) iteration utilizing Dynamic Thresholding—a software-defined algorithm that tracks the environmental noise floor \cite{green2005}. Finally, to overcome severe outdoor solar interference \cite{rajagopal2012}, we introduce a Goertzel algorithm-based digital signal processing (DSP) approach, effectively turning a low-cost microcontroller into a software-defined radio for optical frequencies.

\subsection{Related Works and Novelty}
Standard commercial IR systems avoid ambient noise by utilizing 38kHz Carrier Waves and specialized TSOP receiver modules. While effective, these modules act as "black boxes" that strip the analog signal data away from the microcontroller, preventing custom low-level signal analysis. Conversely, standard unmodulated photodiode circuits use fixed analog thresholds that fail the moment room lighting changes. Our proposed Dynamic Thresholding and Goertzel implementations bridge this gap, retaining raw analog sampling while applying dynamic digital filters to reject ambient DC bias. \subsection{Baud Rate and Telemetry Applicability}
Morse code, utilizing OOK, possesses a fundamentally low baud rate compared to modern digital modulation schemes like UART or QAM. However, for specialized telemetry applications—such as underwater optical communication or low-cost sensor nodes—signal penetration and robustness take precedence over raw speed. The widened tolerance windows used in this paper's decoding logic demonstrate how low baud rate systems can reliably reconstruct highly degraded signals \cite{uysal2015}.

\subsection{Baseband Morse Encoding}
To establish a quantitative baseline for On-Off Keying (OOK) modulation, the system utilizes the standard international Morse code protocol. In this telemetry model, the transmitter's digital output pin toggles between a HIGH (active) and LOW (idle) state to construct characters.

\subsubsection{Hardware Optimization and Adaptive Filtering}
Operating in reverse-bias, the photodiode produces a photocurrent ($I_{pd}$) proportional to the incident photon flux; consequently, the voltage sampled by the ADC is governed by $V_{adc} = I_{pd} \times R$. By increasing $R$ from $10\text{ k}\Omega$ to $1\text{ M}\Omega$, sensitivity was maximized, extending the raw range to 1.57 meters indoors.

To isolate transmission pulses from fluctuating indoor IR noise, we implement a discrete-time Dynamic Thresholding algorithm. The core challenge is that the photodiode's ambient baseline is not zero; it is a floating DC offset that shifts as environmental lighting changes. To track this, the receiver converts the continuous-time analog voltage $x(t)$ into a discrete-time sequence by polling the Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) at designated intervals. Let $T_s$ represent this sampling period. The discrete sample index $n$ maps directly to physical continuous time via the relation $t = n T_s$, defining our sampled sequence as $x[n] = x(n T_s)$. 

The algorithm calculates a rolling local mean, over a circular buffer of $N$ samples to capture the slow-moving ambient noise floor:
\begin{equation}
    \tau[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} x[n-i] + \sigma \label{eq:thres}
\end{equation}

This moving average inherently acts as a digital low-pass filter. To extract the high-frequency OOK pulses, a dynamic threshold $\tau[n]$ is established by adding a constant margin $\sigma$ to the local mean:

By dynamically adjusting the threshold relative to the moving baseline, the detection zone floats alongside ambient light shifts. The logic implementation is detailed in Algorithm \ref{alg:dynamic_threshold}, and the discrete sampling behavior is visualized in Fig. \ref{fig:dynamic_graph}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Dynamic Thresholding for OOK Demodulation}
\label{alg:dynamic_threshold}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Discrete input sequence $x[n]$, Window size $N$, Margin $\sigma$
\State Initialize circular buffer $B$ of size $N$ to 0
\State Initialize running sum $S \leftarrow 0$
\For{each strictly timed sampling interval $n$}
    \State $S \leftarrow S - B[n \bmod N]$ \Comment{Remove oldest sample}
    \State $B[n \bmod N] \leftarrow x[n]$ \Comment{Insert new sample}
    \State $S \leftarrow S + x[n]$
    \State $\mu[n] \leftarrow S / N$ \Comment{Calculate local mean}
    \State $\tau[n] \leftarrow \mu[n] + \sigma$ \Comment{Set floating threshold}
    \If{$x[n] > \tau[n]$}
        \State Register logical HIGH (Pulse active)
    \Else
        \State Register logical LOW (Idle)
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Amplitude Modulation}

\subsection{Limitations of Baseband Detection in Sunlight}
While unmodulated IR transmission functions indoors, it fails in direct sunlight. The sun acts as a continuous, high-intensity IR source that drives a massive steady-state photocurrent, saturating the receiver's 10-bit ADC and eliminating the dynamic range needed to detect Morse pulses. 

To prevent this "hard" saturation outdoors, the receiver's load resistor can be decreased (e.g., from $1\text{M}\Omega$ to $10\text{k}\Omega$). While this keeps the solar DC component within measurable bounds, it severely degrades the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), reducing the intentional pulses to minute fluctuations on a massive DC floor. Recovering this attenuated signal requires transitioning from baseband amplitude tracking to frequency-selective filtering.

\subsection{Modulation}
To isolate the data from the solar baseline, we implement On-Off Keying (OOK), a fundamental form of Amplitude Modulation (AM). Instead of emitting a static DC pulse, the transmitter modulates the IR LED with a 2~kHz square wave carrier during every active Morse symbol.

This modulation shifts the transmission into the frequency domain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DC Offset (Sun):} Solar interference remains concentrated at 0~Hz.
    \item \textbf{Data Signal:} The Morse pulses are nested inside a 2000~Hz carrier band.
\end{itemize}
By toggling the LED at 2~kHz, the receiver can selectively filter out the 0~Hz solar bias and evaluate only the oscillating AC energy. Operating at a 50\% duty cycle also halves the active transmission power, extending battery life for remote nodes.

\subsection{Demodulation}

\subsubsection{Receiver}
To detect the 2~kHz carrier digitally, the ADC prescaler is reduced from 128 to 16, accelerating the sampling rate ($f_s$) to $\approx 60\text{ kHz}$. This massive oversampling provides the resolution necessary for the Goertzel algorithm \cite{smith1997}, which we utilize as a computationally lightweight digital bandpass filter. 

The receiver pipeline is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:modulated_block}. For a given target frequency index $k$ and block size $N$, the algorithm computes the intermediate state $y[n]$ for each incoming sample $x[n]$ using the second-order difference equations \cite{goertzel}:
\begin{align}
    s[n] &= x[n] + 2 \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{N}\right) s[n-1] - s[n-2] \\
    y[n] &= s[n] - e^{-j\omega_{0}} s[n-1]
\end{align}
where $y[-1] = y[-2] = 0$.

After processing $N$ samples, the squared spectral magnitude $M^2$ at the target frequency is extracted:
\begin{align}
    M^2 &= y^2[N-1] + y^2[N-2] \\ 
	&- 2 \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{N}\right) y[N-1] y[N-2]
\end{align}
Using the squared magnitude bypasses the computational cost of floating-point square roots. 

A block size of $N=80$ provides a processing window of $\approx 1.33\text{ ms}$, ensuring temporal responsiveness for decoding Morse timings. This creates a bin resolution ($\Delta f = f_s / N$) of $750\text{ Hz}$. The index $k = \text{round}(N \cdot f_{target} / f_s) = 3$ centers the filter at $2250\text{ Hz}$. This wide main lobe comfortably captures the $2000\text{ Hz}$ carrier while aggressively suppressing out-of-band signals like 0~Hz DC sunlight and 120~Hz AC flicker.

\subsubsection{Static Thresholding and OOK Demodulation}
While $M^2$ theoretically approaches zero during transmission gaps, ADC quantization and thermal noise establish a low-level baseline. We apply an empirical static threshold ($\tau_{mag} = 15000$) to digitize the signal. 

Because constructive interference from a valid 2~kHz carrier produces $M^2$ values on the order of $10^5$, this threshold safely clears the noise floor while maintaining sensitivity to distant transmissions. The real-time OOK demodulation operates as a binary slicer:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Pulse Detected} = \begin{cases} 
      \text{True (HIGH)}, & \text{if } M^2 > \tau_{mag} \\
      \text{False (LOW)}, & \text{otherwise} 
   \end{cases}
\end{equation}
By filtering out the wandering DC bias mathematically, the Goertzel algorithm ensures absolute baseline stability. This eliminates the need for complex adaptive thresholding and allows robust OOK demodulation under variable lighting conditions.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This paper demonstrated a systematic progression in overcoming the physical layer limitations of low-cost optical wireless communication. By evolving from basic visible light sensing to an adaptive Infrared system, we mitigated photo-conductive latency and indoor thermal noise. By shifting the complexity from hardware to a software-defined paradigm—utilizing 2~kHz OOK modulation and an optimized Goertzel digital filter—the system successfully maintained 100\% decoding accuracy at ranges up to 2m, even under direct solar interference. This "virtual filter" approach highlights the potential for low-power microcontrollers to perform sophisticated signal recovery in high-noise environments without requiring specialized commercial hardware. Future work will explore increasing the baud rate by optimizing the Goertzel block size $N$ for higher-speed telemetry.
