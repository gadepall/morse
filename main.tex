\section{Introduction}
Wireless optical communication is highly dependent on clear Line-of-Sight (LoS) and a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) \cite{elgala2011, ghassemlooy2012}. In low-cost embedded systems, a standard Light Emitting Diode (LED) paired with a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) is commonly used to demonstrate data transfer. However, ambient environmental lighting introduces a severe DC bias into the receiver, and LDRs exhibit significant photo-conductive latency \cite{sewaiwar2015}. 
The novelty of this paper lies in its evolutionary approach to solving physical layer limitations, building upon foundational On-Off Keying (OOK) optical paradigms \cite{komine2004}. We first establish a baseline VLC system to document hardware-induced signal degradation, known as the "Iceberg Effect." Following this, we introduce an advanced Infrared (IR) iteration utilizing Dynamic Thresholding—a software-defined algorithm that tracks the environmental noise floor \cite{green2005}. Finally, to overcome severe outdoor solar interference \cite{rajagopal2012}, we introduce a Goertzel algorithm-based digital signal processing (DSP) approach, effectively turning a low-cost microcontroller into a software-defined radio for optical frequencies.

\subsection{Related Works and Novelty}
Standard commercial IR systems avoid ambient noise by utilizing 38kHz Carrier Waves and specialized TSOP receiver modules. While effective, these modules act as "black boxes" that strip the analog signal data away from the microcontroller, preventing custom low-level signal analysis. Conversely, standard unmodulated photodiode circuits use fixed analog thresholds that fail the moment room lighting changes. Our proposed Dynamic Thresholding and Goertzel implementations bridge this gap, retaining raw analog sampling while applying dynamic digital filters to reject ambient DC bias. Morse code, utilizing OOK, possesses a fundamentally low baud rate compared to modern digital modulation schemes like UART or QAM. \subsection{Infrared Evolution and Dynamic Thresholding}

\subsubsection{Hardware Optimization and Adaptive Filtering}
Operating in reverse-bias, the photodiode produces a photocurrent ($I_{pd}$) proportional to the incident photon flux; consequently, the voltage sampled by the ADC is governed by $V_{adc} = I_{pd} \times R$. By increasing $R$ from $10\text{ k}\Omega$ to $1\text{ M}\Omega$, sensitivity was maximized, extending the raw range to 1.57 meters indoors.

To isolate transmission pulses from fluctuating indoor IR noise, we implement a discrete-time Dynamic Thresholding algorithm. The core challenge is that the photodiode's ambient baseline is not zero; it is a floating DC offset that shifts as environmental lighting changes. To track this, the receiver converts the continuous-time analog voltage $x(t)$ into a discrete-time sequence by polling the Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) at designated intervals. Let $T_s$ represent this sampling period. The discrete sample index $n$ maps directly to physical continuous time via the relation $t = n T_s$, defining our sampled sequence as $x[n] = x(n T_s)$. 

The algorithm calculates a rolling local mean, over a circular buffer of $N$ samples to capture the slow-moving ambient noise floor:
\begin{equation}
    \tau[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} x[n-i] + \sigma \label{eq:thres}
\end{equation}

This moving average inherently acts as a digital low-pass filter. To extract the high-frequency OOK pulses, a dynamic threshold $\tau[n]$ is established by adding a constant margin $\sigma$ to the local mean:

By dynamically adjusting the threshold relative to the moving baseline, the detection zone floats alongside ambient light shifts. The logic implementation is detailed in Algorithm \ref{alg:dynamic_threshold}, and the discrete sampling behavior is visualized in Fig. \ref{fig:dynamic_graph}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Dynamic Thresholding for OOK Demodulation}
\label{alg:dynamic_threshold}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Discrete input sequence $x[n]$, Window size $N$, Margin $\sigma$
\State Initialize circular buffer $B$ of size $N$ to 0
\State Initialize running sum $S \leftarrow 0$
\For{each strictly timed sampling interval $n$}
    \State $S \leftarrow S - B[n \bmod N]$ \Comment{Remove oldest sample}
    \State $B[n \bmod N] \leftarrow x[n]$ \Comment{Insert new sample}
    \State $S \leftarrow S + x[n]$
    \State $\mu[n] \leftarrow S / N$ \Comment{Calculate local mean}
    \State $\tau[n] \leftarrow \mu[n] + \sigma$ \Comment{Set floating threshold}
    \If{$x[n] > \tau[n]$}
        \State Register logical HIGH (Pulse active)
    \Else
        \State Register logical LOW (Idle)
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Frequency Modulation and Software-Defined Filtering}

\subsubsection{Limitations in Direct Sunlight and Hardware Gain Control}
While the initial IR setup successfully mitigated indoor noise, it typically fails in direct sunlight. The sun acts as a high-intensity IR emitter, causing the photodiode to produce a massive steady-state photocurrent. This threatens to saturate the 10-bit ADC at a constant 1023, effectively "blinding" the receiver and removing the dynamic range necessary for processing.

To prevent "hard" saturation, the receiver's load resistor ($R$) is decreased (e.g., from $1\text{M}\Omega$ to $10\text{k}\Omega$) when operating outdoors. While this keeps the DC component within the ADC's measurable range, it significantly reduces the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), as the intentional AC pulses become minute fluctuations on a massive DC floor. This necessitates a transition to a frequency-domain detection approach to isolate the signal. 

By reducing $R$, we trade off raw voltage swing for headroom, relying on the high-gain nature of the Goertzel algorithm to recover the attenuated signal from the resulting narrow dynamic range.

\subsubsection{2~kHz On-Off Keying (OOK) Modulation}
To resolve solar saturation, we transition to a Software-Defined Optical paradigm. Instead of a static DC pulse, the transmitter modulates the IR LED with a 2~kHz square wave carrier during every Morse "mark" (dit or dah).

This process, acting as a form of subcarrier modulation, involves gating the continuous DC transmission pulse with a high-frequency sequence. By toggling the LED at 2~kHz (a 50\% duty cycle), we effectively perform a frequency-domain shift:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DC Offset (Sun):} The solar interference resides at 0Hz.
    \item \textbf{Data Signal:} The intentional Morse pulses are now "nested" inside a 2000Hz carrier.
\end{itemize}
This separation allows the receiver to ignore the saturated 0Hz baseline and focus entirely on the energy oscillating at the target frequency band. \\
Furthermore, operating the LED at a 50\% duty cycle reduces the active transmission power consumption by half compared to the continuous DC pulses observed during the Baseband IR, highly benefiting battery-constrained remote sensor nodes.

\subsubsection{Sampling and Goertzel Digital Signal Processing}
To detect the 2~kHz carrier without external hardware filters, the receiver's ADC must satisfy the Nyquist theorem (requiring a minimum sampling rate of 4~kHz). We modify the \texttt{ADCSRA} register to change the prescaler from 128 to 16, accelerating the sampling rate from $\approx 10\text{ kHz}$ to $\approx 60\text{ kHz}$, which provides massive oversampling for high-resolution frequency detection.

We then implement the Goertzel algorithm \cite{smith1997} to act as a digital bandpass filter. Unlike a full Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) that computes all frequency bins, the Goertzel algorithm is computationally optimized to evaluate the spectral energy of a single target frequency ($f_{target} = 2000\text{ Hz}$). Mathematically, it operates similarly to a second-order Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter. It effectively convolves the input signal with a complex sinusoid at the target frequency; target frequencies constructively interfere and accumulate, while off-target frequencies (like 0~Hz DC sunlight or 120~Hz AC flicker) destructively cancel out over the integration period. 

The procedure is detailed in Algorithm \ref{alg:goertzel}. The variable $M^2$ represents the squared spectral magnitude (or power) of the signal at the target frequency. We intentionally evaluate the squared magnitude $M^2$ rather than the absolute magnitude $M$ to avoid the high computational cost of floating-point square root operations on an 8-bit microcontroller.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Real-Time Goertzel Single-Bin DFT}
\label{alg:goertzel}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $N$ (Block size, e.g., 80)
\Require $k$ (Target frequency index)
\Require $x[n]$ (Incoming ADC samples)
\State \textbf{Initialization:}
\State $\omega \gets \frac{2 \pi k}{N}$
\State $\text{coeff} \gets 2 \cos(\omega)$
\State $q_1 \gets 0$
\State $q_2 \gets 0$
\Statex
\State \textbf{Processing Loop (Per Block):}
\For{$n = 0$ to $N - 1$}
    \State $q_0 \gets \text{coeff} \cdot q_1 - q_2 + x[n]$
    \State $q_2 \gets q_1$
    \State $q_1 \gets q_0$
\EndFor
\Statex
\State \textbf{Magnitude Evaluation:}
\State $M^2 \gets q_1^2 + q_2^2 - (\text{coeff} \cdot q_1 \cdot q_2)$
\State \Return $M^2$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

After $N=80$ samples (which takes approximately $1.33\text{ ms}$ to process at 60~kHz), the squared magnitude $M^2$ is evaluated against a static threshold. Because DC sunlight and indoor flicker do not resonate with the pre-computed coefficient, their resultant magnitude approaches zero. This effectively neutralizes the moving noise floor that typically requires adaptive dynamic thresholding in IR communications, allowing this Frequency-Modulated system to reliably "see" the 2~kHz pulses even through a saturated DC bias.

\subsubsection{Static Thresholding and OOK Demodulation}
In an ideal, noiseless environment, the Goertzel algorithm would compute a squared magnitude of exactly $M^2 = 0$ when the 2~kHz transmitter is off. In practice, however, the receiver's baseline is subject to thermal noise, ADC quantization errors, and residual harmonics from ambient electromagnetic interference. Consequently, the quiescent state continuously yields random, low-level spectral noise.

To translate the continuous Goertzel output into a discrete binary sequence, an empirical static threshold ($\tau_{mag}$) must be established to differentiate between ambient noise and a legitimate 2~kHz pulse. In this implementation, the threshold is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{mag} = 15000
\end{equation}

Because the algorithm accumulates 80 samples of 10-bit ADC values, constructive interference during a valid 2~kHz pulse produces massive squared magnitudes, typically on the order of $10^5$ to $10^6$. The threshold $\tau_{mag} = 15000$ is positioned sufficiently high to safely clear the baseline noise floor (which hovers significantly lower), yet low enough to detect attenuated signals from distant transmitters.

During the processing loop, the system acts as a digital slicer for On-Off Keying (OOK) demodulation by evaluating the boolean condition:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Pulse Detected} = \begin{cases} 
      \text{True (HIGH)}, & \text{if } M^2 > \tau_{mag} \\
      \text{False (LOW)}, & \text{otherwise} 
   \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Crucially, this approach eliminates the need for the adaptive moving-average buffer required in raw unmodulated IR detection. Because the Goertzel filter mathematically isolates the target frequency and inherently suppresses the 0~Hz DC offset from sunlight, the baseline no longer wanders. This stability allows the system to rely on a highly efficient, hardcoded static threshold without risking false positives from environmental lighting shifts.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This paper demonstrated a systematic progression in overcoming the physical layer limitations of low-cost optical wireless communication. By evolving from basic visible light sensing to an adaptive Infrared system, we mitigated photo-conductive latency and indoor thermal noise. By shifting the complexity from hardware to a software-defined paradigm—utilizing 2~kHz OOK modulation and an optimized Goertzel digital filter—the system successfully maintained 100\% decoding accuracy at ranges up to 2m, even under direct solar interference. This "virtual filter" approach highlights the potential for low-power microcontrollers to perform sophisticated signal recovery in high-noise environments without requiring specialized commercial hardware. Future work will explore increasing the baud rate by optimizing the Goertzel block size $N$ for higher-speed telemetry.
