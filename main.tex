\section{Introduction}
Wireless optical communication is highly dependent on clear Line-of-Sight (LoS) and a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) \cite{elgala2011, ghassemlooy2012}. In entry-level embedded systems, a standard Light Emitting Diode (LED) paired with a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) is commonly used to demonstrate data transfer. However, ambient environmental lighting introduces a severe DC bias into the receiver, and LDRs exhibit significant photo-conductive latency \cite{sewaiwar2015}.

The novelty of this paper lies in its evolutionary approach to solving these physical layer limitations, building upon foundational On-Off Keying (OOK) optical paradigms \cite{komine2004}. We first establish a baseline VLC system to document the hardware-induced signal degradation known as the ``Iceberg Effect.'' Following this, we introduce an advanced Infrared (IR) iteration. The core innovation of this second phase is the Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM), a software-defined signal processing algorithm that tracks the environmental noise floor \cite{green2005}. Finally, to overcome severe outdoor solar interference \cite{rajagopal2012}, we introduce a Goertzel algorithm-based digital signal processing (DSP) approach, effectively turning the low-cost microcontroller into a software-defined radio for optical frequencies.

\subsection{Related Works and Novelty}
Standard commercial IR systems avoid ambient noise by utilizing 38kHz Carrier Waves and specialized TSOP receiver modules. While effective, these modules act as ``black boxes'' that strip the analog signal data away from the microcontroller, preventing custom low-level signal analysis. Conversely, standard unmodulated photodiode circuits use fixed analog thresholds that fail the moment room lighting changes. Our proposed SDM and Goertzel implementations bridge this gap, retaining raw analog sampling while applying dynamic digital filters to reject ambient DC bias. \subsection{Baud Rate and Telemetry Applicability}
Morse code, utilizing OOK, possesses a fundamentally low baud rate compared to modern digital modulation schemes like UART or QAM. However, for specialized telemetry applications—such as underwater optical communication or low-cost sensor nodes—signal penetration and robustness take precedence over raw speed. The widened tolerance windows used in this paper's decoding logic demonstrate how low baud rate systems can reliably reconstruct highly degraded signals \cite{uysal2015}.

\subsection{Baseband Morse Encoding}
To establish a quantitative baseline for On-Off Keying (OOK) modulation, the system utilizes the standard international Morse code protocol. In this telemetry model, the transmitter's digital output pin toggles between a HIGH (active) and LOW (idle) state to construct characters. 

The protocol defines all signal durations relative to a single base time unit ($T_u$), which in our experimental setup is set to $200\text{ ms}$. The strict timing rules governing this variable-length baseband modulation are detailed in Table \ref{tab:morse_timing}.

\section{Phase I: Visible Light Baseline}

\subsection{Circuit Implementation}
The baseline system operates entirely in the visible spectrum. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item {Transmitter (TX):} An Arduino microcontroller drives a standard visible LED. The LED's anode connects to Digital Pin 5, while the cathode is grounded through a $220\Omega$ current-limiting resistor. 
    \item {Receiver (RX):} The optical sensor is a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) arranged in a voltage divider. The 5V supply is connected to one leg of the LDR. The secondary leg connects to the Arduino's Analog Pin A0 and a $10\text{k}\Omega$ pull-down resistor connected to ground. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware Latency and The ``Iceberg Effect''}
When bridging analog optical sensing and digital microcontrollers, signal truncation occurs. As shown in Fig. \ref{fig:iceberg}, an expected ``Dah'' signal transmitted for 600ms is detected for a fraction of that time. LDRs exhibit notable rise/fall latency, resulting in a trapezoidal curve. To counteract this, our receiver employs widened tolerance windows.

\subsection{Visible Light Results}
The baseline system achieved a maximum reliable transmission distance of only 11-12 cm in room light, and 27 cm in total darkness.

\section{Phase II: Infrared Evolution and SDM}

\subsection{Circuit Implementation}
To resolve the noise and range issues of Phase I, the physical layer was upgraded to a 940nm Infrared (IR) ecosystem.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transmitter (TX):} The visible LED is replaced by an IR LED. 
    \item \textbf{Receiver (RX) Sensor:} A high-speed IR photodiode replaces the LDR, operating in reverse-bias mode. The cathode connects to 5V, and the anode connects to Analog Pin A0 and a pull-down load resistor ($R_L$) to ground.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware Optimization and SDM}
Operating in reverse-bias, the photodiode produces a photocurrent ($I_{pd}$) proportional to the incident photon flux; consequently, the voltage sampled by the ADC is governed by $V_{adc} = I_{pd} \times R_L$. By increasing $R_L$ from $10\text{k}\Omega$ to $1\text{M}\Omega$, sensitivity was maximized, extending the raw range to 1.57 meters indoors.

To isolate the transmission pulses from amplified indoor thermal noise, we developed the Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM) \cite{green2005}. The algorithm maintains a rolling buffer of $N=10$ readings to calculate a floating real-time average ($V_{noise}$):
\begin{align}
    V_{noise}[n] &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} V_{adc}[n-i] \label{eq:noise} \\
    V_{threshold}[n] &= V_{noise}[n] + \sigma \label{eq:thresh}
\end{align}
Where $\sigma$ is a signal margin constant ensuring only intentional peaks surpass the baseline.

\section{Phase III: High-Solar Environments and Software-Defined Filtering}

\subsection{Limitations in Direct Sunlight}
While the Phase II setup successfully mitigated indoor noise, it fails in direct sunlight. The sun acts as a high-intensity IR emitter, causing the photodiode to produce a massive steady-state photocurrent. This saturates the 10-bit ADC at 1023, effectively ``blinding'' the receiver and removing the dynamic range necessary for the SDM algorithm to function.

\subsection{2kHz On-Off Keying (OOK) Modulation}
To resolve solar saturation, we transition to a Software-Defined Optical paradigm. Instead of a static DC pulse, the transmitter modulates the IR LED with a 2kHz square wave carrier during every Morse ``mark'' (dit or dah).

The term \textbf{``chopping''} refers to this process of breaking a continuous DC signal into a high-frequency sequence of pulses. By toggling the LED at 2kHz (a 50\% duty cycle), we perform a frequency-domain shift:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DC Offset (Sun):} The solar interference resides at 0Hz.
    \item \textbf{Data Signal:} The intentional Morse pulses are now ``nested'' inside a 2000Hz carrier.
\end{itemize}
This separation allows the receiver to ignore the saturated 0Hz baseline and focus entirely on the energy oscillating at the target frequency band. This is implemented in software using the \texttt{tone()} function, which leverages the microcontroller's internal timers to generate a precise square wave.
Furthermore, operating the LED at a 50% duty cycle reduces the active transmission power consumption by half compared to the continuous DC pulses of Phase II, highly benefiting battery-constrained remote sensor nodes.

\subsection{Sampling and Goertzel Digital Signal Processing}
To detect the 2kHz carrier without external hardware filters, the receiver's ADC must satisfy the Nyquist theorem (requiring a minimum sampling rate of 4kHz). We modify the \texttt{ADCSRA} register to change the prescaler from 128 to 16, accelerating the sampling rate from $\approx 10\text{kHz}$ to $\approx 60\text{kHz}$, which provides massive oversampling for high-resolution frequency detection.

We then implement the Goertzel algorithm \cite{smith1997} to act as a digital bandpass filter. Unlike a full Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), the Goertzel algorithm is computationally optimized to calculate the magnitude of a single target frequency ($f_{target} = 2000\text{Hz}$), making it ideal for the limited memory of microcontrollers \cite{mitra2006}. 

During the real-time loop, the system updates a second-order Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter state:
\begin{equation}
    s[n] = x[n] + \left(2 \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{N}\right) \cdot s[n-1]\right) - s[n-2]
\end{equation}
After $N=80$ samples (which takes approximately $1.33\text{ ms}$ to process at 60kHz), the squared magnitude $M^2$ is evaluated. Because DC sunlight (0Hz) and 120Hz indoor flicker do not resonate with the pre-computed coefficient, their resultant magnitude approaches zero. This effectively neutralizes the moving noise floor that required adaptive SDM in Phase II, allowing Phase III to utilize a highly reliable static magnitude threshold to ``see'' the 2kHz flashes even through a saturated DC bias.

\section{Experimental Results}

\subsection{Throughput and Bitrate Analysis}
We evaluated the efficiency of the three phases by transmitting a 44-byte (352-bit) test sentence. While the Phase I (LDR) setup required slower custom timings (101.8s) to account for photo-conductive latency, the transition to standard Morse timing in Phase II and Phase III significantly improved throughput. Because Morse code utilizes variable-length OOK encoding, instantaneous throughput fluctuates based on character transmission length. A 44-byte pangram was explicitly chosen as the test payload to force the transmission of all alphabet symbol variants, thereby providing a normalized, worst-case representative average for system bitrate.

The integration of impedance-tuned hardware with software-defined filtering yielded profound improvements. As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:graph}, the visible light baseline failed abruptly at 27 cm. Decoding accuracy was empirically measured as the percentage of correctly reconstructed characters from the 44-byte test payload at varying distances.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
This paper presented an evolutionary approach to overcoming the physical layer limitations of low-cost optical wireless communication. By systematically advancing from a basic visible light LDR circuit to an Infrared Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM), we mitigated indoor thermal noise and extended range. Ultimately, we addressed the critical challenge of direct solar DC saturation by transforming the microcontroller into a software-defined radio. Utilizing a 2kHz OOK modulation scheme paired with an optimized Goertzel digital bandpass filter, the system successfully isolated optical telemetry from severe ambient interference without relying on specialized commercial hardware filters.
