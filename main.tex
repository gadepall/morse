\title{Adaptive Optical Telemetry: Evolving from Visible Light Baselines to Infrared Stepwise Derivative Thresholding and Goertzel Filtering}

\author{Kartik Lahoti, Puni Aditya \\
\textit{Department of Electrical Engineering} \\
\textit{IIT Hyderabad}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Optical wireless communication offers a license-free alternative to Radio Frequency (RF) systems. However, simple visible light links utilizing Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs) face significant challenges regarding ambient light interference and hardware latency. This paper outlines a comprehensive multi-stage development of a Morse Code On-Off Keying (OOK) system. Phase I establishes a baseline Visible Light Communication (VLC) link, documenting a maximum range of 27 cm and identifying the ``Iceberg Effect'' of signal truncation. Phase II introduces the transition to the Infrared (IR) spectrum coupled with a Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM) for adaptive thresholding, extending the range to 1.57 meters indoors. Finally, Phase III addresses the critical limitation of direct solar interference by implementing a fully software-defined 2kHz carrier wave and a digital Goertzel filter, successfully rejecting DC solar saturation without requiring complex commercial hardware filters.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Visible Light Communication (VLC), Infrared (IR), Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM), Goertzel Algorithm, On-Off Keying (OOK), Adaptive Thresholding.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
Wireless optical communication is highly dependent on clear Line-of-Sight (LoS) and a high Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) \cite{elgala2011, ghassemlooy2012}. In entry-level embedded systems, a standard Light Emitting Diode (LED) paired with a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) is commonly used to demonstrate data transfer. However, ambient environmental lighting introduces a severe DC bias into the receiver, and LDRs exhibit significant photo-conductive latency \cite{sewaiwar2015}.

The novelty of this paper lies in its evolutionary approach to solving these physical layer limitations. We first establish a baseline VLC system to document the hardware-induced signal degradation known as the ``Iceberg Effect.'' Following this, we introduce an advanced Infrared (IR) iteration. The core innovation of this second phase is the Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM), a software-defined signal processing algorithm that tracks the environmental noise floor. Finally, to overcome severe outdoor solar interference, we introduce a Goertzel algorithm-based digital signal processing (DSP) approach, effectively turning the low-cost microcontroller into a software-defined radio for optical frequencies.

\subsection{Related Works and Novelty}
Standard commercial IR systems avoid ambient noise by utilizing 38kHz Carrier Waves and specialized TSOP receiver modules. While effective, these modules act as ``black boxes'' that strip the analog signal data away from the microcontroller, preventing custom low-level signal analysis. Conversely, standard unmodulated photodiode circuits use fixed analog thresholds that fail the moment room lighting changes. Our proposed SDM and Goertzel implementations bridge this gap, retaining raw analog sampling while applying dynamic digital filters to reject ambient DC bias. 

\subsection{Baud Rate and Telemetry Applicability}
Morse code, utilizing OOK, possesses a fundamentally low baud rate compared to modern digital modulation schemes like UART or QAM. However, for specialized telemetry applications—such as underwater optical communication or low-cost sensor nodes—signal penetration and robustness take precedence over raw speed. The widened tolerance windows used in this paper's decoding logic demonstrate how low baud rate systems can reliably reconstruct highly degraded signals \cite{uysal2015}.

\section{Phase I: Visible Light Baseline}

\subsection{Circuit Implementation}
The baseline system operates entirely in the visible spectrum. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transmitter (TX):} An Arduino microcontroller drives a standard visible LED. The LED's anode connects to Digital Pin 5, while the cathode grounds through a $220\Omega$ current-limiting resistor. 
    \item \textbf{Receiver (RX):} The optical sensor is a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) arranged in a voltage divider. The 5V supply is connected to one leg of the LDR. The secondary leg connects to the Arduino's Analog Pin A0 and a $10\text{k}\Omega$ pull-down resistor connected to ground. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware Latency and The ``Iceberg Effect''}
When bridging analog optical sensing and digital microcontrollers, signal truncation occurs. As shown in Fig. \ref{fig:iceberg}, an expected ``Dah'' signal transmitted for 600ms is detected for a fraction of that time. LDRs exhibit notable rise/fall latency, resulting in a trapezoidal curve. To counteract this, our receiver employs widened tolerance windows.

\subsection{Visible Light Results}
The baseline system achieved a maximum reliable transmission distance of only 11-12 cm in room light, and 27 cm in total darkness.

\section{Phase II: Infrared Evolution and SDM}

\subsection{Circuit Implementation}
To resolve the noise and range issues of Phase I, the physical layer was upgraded to a 940nm Infrared (IR) ecosystem.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transmitter (TX):} The visible LED is replaced by an IR LED. 
    \item \textbf{Receiver (RX) Sensor:} A high-speed IR photodiode replaces the LDR, operating in reverse-bias mode. The cathode connects to 5V, and the anode connects to Analog Pin A0 and a pull-down load resistor ($R_L$) to ground.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware Optimization and SDM}
Because a photodiode in reverse-bias produces a photocurrent ($I_{pd}$) proportional to the incident photon flux, the voltage sampled by the ADC is governed by $V_{adc} = I_{pd} \times R_L$. By increasing $R_L$ from $10\text{k}\Omega$ to $1\text{M}\Omega$, sensitivity was maximized, extending the raw range to 1.57 meters indoors.

To isolate the transmission pulses from amplified indoor thermal noise, we developed the Stepwise Derivative Method (SDM). The algorithm maintains a rolling buffer of $N=10$ readings to calculate a floating real-time average ($V_{noise}$):
\begin{align}
    V_{noise}[n] &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} V_{adc}[n-i] \label{eq:noise} \\
    V_{threshold}[n] &= V_{noise}[n] + \sigma \label{eq:thresh}
\end{align}
Where $\sigma$ is a signal margin constant ensuring only intentional peaks surpass the baseline.

\section{Phase III: High-Solar Environments and Software-Defined Filtering}

\subsection{Limitations in Direct Sunlight}
While the Phase II $1\text{M}\Omega$ SDM setup successfully mitigated indoor noise, it catastrophically fails in direct sunlight. The sun acts as a massive IR emitter. The intense ambient solar photocurrent multiplied by the high $1\text{M}\Omega$ trans-impedance causes the voltage to instantly saturate the microcontroller's 10-bit ADC at its maximum value of 1023. Consequently, the SDM algorithm's threshold calculation breaks, as it cannot mathematically exceed the hardware ceiling.

\subsection{Software-Defined Modulation}
To resolve solar saturation, we transition to a Software-Defined Optical Communication paradigm. Instead of illuminating the IR LED statically during a Morse symbol, the transmitter modulates the LED at a distinct frequency of $2\text{kHz}$ . 

To fulfill the Nyquist sampling theorem without external hardware filters, the receiver's internal ADC prescaler register (\texttt{ADCSRA}) is modified from 128 to 16. This accelerates the sampling rate from $\approx 10\text{kHz}$ to $\approx 60\text{kHz}$, providing sufficient temporal resolution to read the $2\text{kHz}$ carrier wave.

\subsection{The Goertzel Algorithm (DSP)}
To extract the $2\text{kHz}$ carrier from the saturated solar DC bias, we implemented the Goertzel algorithm \cite{smith1997}. Unlike a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) that computes the entire frequency spectrum, the Goertzel algorithm is mathematically optimized to calculate the magnitude of a single target frequency, making it highly efficient for embedded systems.

Let $f_s$ be the auto-calibrated sampling frequency, $f_{target} = 2000\text{ Hz}$, and $N = 80$ be the block size. The system pre-computes the target frequency bin ($k$), the angular frequency ($\omega$), and a real coefficient ($C$):

\begin{align}
    k &= \text{round}\left( N \frac{f_{target}}{f_s} \right) \\
    \omega &= \frac{2 \pi k}{N} \\
    C &= 2 \cos(\omega)
\end{align}

During the real-time processing loop, the Arduino samples the ADC ($x[n]$) and updates a second-order Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter state . It only retains the two previous states, drastically reducing memory overhead:

\begin{equation}
    s[n] = x[n] + (C \cdot s[n-1]) - s[n-2]
\end{equation}

After $N$ samples are processed, the squared magnitude ($M^2$) of the target frequency is evaluated:

\begin{equation}
    M^2 = s[N-1]^2 + s[N-2]^2 - (C \cdot s[N-1] \cdot s[N-2])
\end{equation}

Because DC sunlight ($0\text{ Hz}$) and AC room lighting ($120\text{ Hz}$) do not resonate with the pre-computed coefficient $C$, their resultant magnitude $M^2$ approaches zero. Only the specific $2\text{kHz}$ IR flashes generate a massive magnitude spike, allowing the system to completely reject solar interference mathematically.

\section{Experimental Results and Conclusion}
The integration of impedance-tuned hardware and DSP software yielded profound improvements. As illustrated in Fig. \ref{fig:graph}, the visible light baseline failed abruptly at 27 cm. The unoptimized IR setup ($10\text{k}\Omega$) reached 47 cm. The fully optimized IR-SDM setup ($1\text{M}\Omega$) successfully maintained 100\% decoding accuracy up to 157 cm indoors. Furthermore, the Phase III Goertzel algorithm proved that low-cost microcontrollers can effectively reject severe solar DC bias without requiring specialized TSOP hardware filters, paving the way for robust, software-defined optical telemetry.
